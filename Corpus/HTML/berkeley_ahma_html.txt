                          <h1 class="title" id="page-title">AHMA Seminars</h1>
                                                        


<article class="node-216 node node-page view-mode-full clearfix" about="/ahma-seminars" typeof="foaf:Document">

      <header>
                  <span property="dc:title" content="AHMA Seminars" class="rdf-meta element-hidden"></span>
      
          </header>
  
  <div class="field field-name-field-image-opt field-type-image field-label-hidden clearfix">
    <div class="field-items">
          <div class="field-item even"><a href="http://ahma.berkeley.edu/sites/default/files/pages/berlin_pergamon.jpg" title="" class="colorbox" rel="gallery-node-216"><img typeof="foaf:Image" src="http://ahma.berkeley.edu/sites/default/files/styles/node_main/public/pages/berlin_pergamon.jpg?itok=2mTeuoB4" alt="" title="" /></a></div>
      </div>
</div>
<div class="field field-name-body field-type-text-with-summary field-label-hidden"><div class="field-items"><div class="field-item even" property="content:encoded"><p>All AHMA students must take one AHMA seminar during Stage I of the program, normally in the first two years. The AHMA seminar is offered annually by two participating faculty members of AHMA who belong to different home departments. The following AHMA seminars will be or have been offered in the recent past.</p>
<p>2017-18: <strong>Greek Economies: The Documentary Evidence</strong>. Professors Emily Mackil (History) and Nikolaos Papazarkadas (Classics). Spring 2018.</p>
<p>2015-16: <strong>Comparative  Urbanism in the Ancient World</strong>. Professors Carlos Noreña (History) and Carol Redmount (Near Eastern Studies). Spring 2016.</p>
<p>2014-15: <strong>Ancient Boiotia: History, Epigraphy, and Culture</strong>. Professors Emily Mackil (History) and Nikolaos Papazarkadas (Classics). Spring 2015.</p>
<p>2013-14: <strong>Etruscan Cities</strong>. Professors Ted Peña (Classics) and L. Pieraccini (Art History). Fall 2013.</p>
<p>2012-13: <strong>The City of Rome: Topography and Urban History.</strong> Professors Carlos Noreña (History) and Ted Peña (Classics). Spring 2013.</p>
<p>2011-12: <strong>Making Things in the Iron Age Levant: An Investigation into Materials, Craft and Production. </strong>Professors Marian Feldman (Art History/NES) and Benjamin Porter (NES)</p>
<p>2010-11: <strong>Greece and the Near East in the Iron Age</strong>. Professors Marian Feldman (Art History/NES) and Andrew Stewart (Art History/Classics)</p>
<p>2009-10: <strong>Sectarianism to Heresiology in Second Temple Judaism</strong><strong>. </strong>Professors Ron Hendel (NES) and Holger Zellentin (Jewish Studies, Graduate Theological Union)</p>
<p>2008-9: <strong>Greek Economies: The Documentary Evidence.</strong> Professors Emily Mackil (History) and Nikolaos Papazarkadas (Classics).</p>
<p>2007-8:  <strong>Regarding Graeco-Roman Egypt: Text and Image, Reading and Viewing in a Multi-Cultural Society</strong> Professors Chris Hallett (Art History) and Todd Hickey (Classics)</p>
</div></div></div>

  <div class="field field-name-field-image-opt field-type-image field-label-hidden clearfix">
    <div class="field-items">
          <div class="field-item even"><a href="http://ahma.berkeley.edu/sites/default/files/pages/epigraphy-of-boeotia-poster.jpg" title="" class="colorbox" rel="gallery-node-221"><img typeof="foaf:Image" src="http://ahma.berkeley.edu/sites/default/files/styles/node_main/public/pages/epigraphy-of-boeotia-poster.jpg?itok=GbW2QbOg" alt="" title="" /></a></div>

<div class="field field-name-body field-type-text-with-summary field-label-hidden"><div class="field-items"><div class="field-item even" property="content:encoded"><p><a href="http://aleshire.berkeley.edu/">The Sara B. Aleshire Center for the Study of Greek Epigraphy</a> organizes and hosts annual (formerly biannual) lectures and seminars in Greek epigraphy.</p>
<p>Occasionally, major conferences on Greek epigraphic matters hosted at Berkeley take the place of the seminar. Thus in 2011 the Aleshire Center organized an  international symposium, <em>The Epigraphy and History of Boeotia: New Finds, New Developments</em>. In January 2016 Berkeley hosted the Second Annual <em>North American Congress of Greek and Latin Epigraphy</em>, a banner event with more than 50 speakers and 140 attendees.</p>
<p> </p>

<div class="field field-name-body field-type-text-with-summary field-label-hidden"><div class="field-items"><div class="field-item even" property="content:encoded"><p>The following is a selection only of courses relating to the Ancient History and Mediterranean Archaeology program that will be offered in the Spring 2014 semester. Please see the <a href="http://schedule.berkeley.edu/" target="_blank">online schedule of classes</a> for updated information.</p>
<p style="padding-left: 30px;"> </p>
<h2>Department of Classics</h2>
<h3 style="padding-left: 30px;">Civil War and Civil Strife in Roman Literature</h3>
<p style="margin-left: 40px;"><strong>Course/Section:</strong> 239 (Section 2)<br />
	 <strong>Location: </strong>308C, Doe Library<br />
	 <strong>Times:</strong> T 2-5p <br /><strong>Instructor:</strong> Sailor, Dylan</p>
<p style="margin-left: 40px;">In this seminar we will look at civil war as a subject of Roman literature. We will read several long stretches of narrative (Caesar BC, Sallust BC, Aeneid 7-12, Lucan, Tacitus’ Histories) as well as a variety of shorter pieces (Vergil, Horace, Propertius, Petronius, etc.). Recurring questions and problems will include (in no particular order) genre, pollution and expiation, literary tradition and competition, language, space, identity, commemoration and forgetting, metaphor, explanation, the Principate, the empire.</p>
<p style="margin-left: 40px;"> </p>
<h3>Vertumnus - A god with no identity, a god with many identities, a god of identity</h3>
<p style="margin-left: 40px;"><strong>Course/Section:</strong> 239 (Section 3)<br />
	 <strong>Location: </strong>308C, Doe Library<br />
	 <strong>Times:</strong> W 2-5p <br /><strong>Instructor:</strong> Bettini, Maurizio</p>
<p style="margin-left: 40px;">The seminar will discuss the role and character of an unusual Roman deity, known to us through one of the most original elegies by Propertius, 4, 2. Vertumnus was a god of metamorphosis – but in which sense? Unlike his Greek counterparts, such as Proteus, Thétis or Thémis, he was not a divine force able to manifest in any possible natural form, such as fire, water, animals, trees ... The metamorphoses of Vertumnus had a specific and limited range, not only the human one, but the one of the civitas. He was a master of social identities, able to fittingly and perfectly take each of them: as such Vertumnus is the perfect guide to introduce ourselves into the very fabric of Roman identity. What did the Romans use to determine / ascertain the identity of a person? The metamorphoses of Vertumnus can reveal that. In addition, we must take into account that Vertumnus’ transformations did not take place only in some imaginary world, that of mythology and literary fiction; but in the middle of the Vicus Tuscus, because, as Propertius tells us, it was also its statue, cast by the legendary artifex Mamurius Veturius, that had the power of turning its external semblance in an endless series of appearances. That will give us an opportunity to explore another fascinating sphere of Roman culture, i. e. the set of semiotic devices used by the Romans to identify their images.</p>
<p style="margin-left: 40px;"> </p>
<h2>Department of History</h2>
<h3>Roman North Africa from 300-500 CE</h3>
<p style="padding-left: 30px;"><strong>Course/Section:</strong> 280A<br />
	 <strong>Location: </strong>3104 Dwinelle<br />
	 <strong>Times:</strong> TH 1-3p <br /><strong>Instructor:</strong> Elm, Susanna</p>
<p style="padding-left: 30px;">North Africa has long been considered a space apart in the Western Roman empire, not least because of its long periods of peace and prosperity. As Brent Shaw's 2011 book on Sacred Violence stresses, however, peace and prosperity were achieved in a context of strive. What then was going on in this province? What does recent research, including archaeological research tell us? How did this part of the empire transform itself into a Christian part, and what are the repercussions? And how different was Africa? Tracing the history of the region between (roughly) the rule of Diocletian and the end of the Vandals should shed some light on a number of important questions, from rural settlements to the decline of Rome even if, inevitably, the figure of Augustine will tower over all else.</p>
<h2><br />
	Department of History of Art</h2>
<h3>The Sacred and the Arts from the Ancient Near East to the Late Antique</h3>
<p style="padding-left: 30px;"><strong>Course/Section:</strong> HA C220<br />
	 <strong>Location:</strong> 308D, Doe Library<br /><strong>Times: </strong>W 9-12 a<br />
	 <strong>Instructors:</strong> Angelova, Diliana — Feldman, Marian</p>
<p style="padding-left: 30px;">This seminar will address cross-cultural and “longue durée” concerns regarding ritual and the sacred in the ancient Near Eastern and Mediterranean world from the early periods in Mesopotamia to the Late Antique (c. 500 CE). We will take as our initial focus several case studies drawn from both the Ancient Near Eastern and Late Antique worlds to serve as springboards for discussion. These case studies will range from landscapes and urban spaces, to individual architectural structures, to images and forms. Topics that will be touched on in the course include spiritual seeing and visuality, mysticism and sacred architecture, visual exegesis, icons and their meanings, material and visual aspects of ritual practice, and the intertwining of the sacred with collective memory and communal identity.</p>
<p style="padding-left: 30px;"> </p>
<h2>Department of Near Eastern Studies</h2>
<h3>Ancient and Modern Hebrew Literary Texts</h3>
<p style="padding-left: 30px;"><strong>Course/Section:</strong> Hebrew 206<br />
	 <strong>Location: </strong>275 BARROWS<br /><strong>Times: </strong>Th 2-5<br />
	 <strong>Instructors:</strong> Hendel, R. S. — Alter, R. B.</p>
<p style="padding-left: 30px;"> </p>
