<p>The <span class="caps">MA</span> is 1 year full-time or 2 years part-time, and currently has 3 compulsory&nbsp;modules:</p>
<ul><li>One covering vernacular Renaissance and research&nbsp;skills</li>
<li>One covering global Renaissances and Intellectual&nbsp;History</li>
<li>A dissertation (18,000&nbsp;words)</li>
</ul><p>Students can then select 2-4 <a href="http://www.ucl.ac.uk/eme/study/early-modern-studies-ma/options">optional modules</a>, based on their academic&nbsp;interests.</p>
<p><span class="caps">CELL</span> o&#xfb00;ers a range of optional modules, though not all are available every&nbsp;year:</p>
<ul><li><a href="http://www.ucl.ac.uk/eme/study/modules/handwriting"><span class="caps">ARTFGE03</span> Early Modern Handwriting and Manuscript Culture for Researchers</a> (15&nbsp;credits)</li>
<li><a href="http://www.ucl.ac.uk/eme/study/modules/books"><span class="caps">ARTFGE04</span> Early Modern Books and Their Readers: Historical Bibliography for Researchers</a> (15&nbsp;credits)</li>
<li><a href="http://www.ucl.ac.uk/eme/study/modules/graduate-it"><span class="caps">EDITG002</span>: <span class="caps">I.T.</span> for Graduate Research</a> (15&nbsp;credits)</li>
<li><a href="courses/cell-ma/latin"><span class="caps">EDITG003</span>: Latin for Reading Knowledge</a> (15&nbsp;credits)</li>

<li><a href="http://www.ucl.ac.uk/eme/study/modules/web01"><span class="caps">EDITG005</span>: Web 0.1: Early Modern Information Culture, c.1450-c.1750</a> (15&nbsp;credits)</li>
<li><a href="http://www.livesandletters.ac.uk/courses/cell-ma/confessional-cultures"><span class="caps">EDITG006</span>: Confessional Cultures in the Dutch Republic <span class="amp">&amp;</span> England, c.1500-c.1700</a> (15&nbsp;credits)</li>

                    <h1 class="title" id="page-title">
          Latin for Reading Knowledge        </h1>
                          <div class="tabs">
                  </div>
                          <div class="region region-content">
    <div id="block-system-main" class="block block-system">

    
  <div class="content">
    <div id="node-127" class="node node-course node-full clearfix" about="/courses/cell-ma/latin" typeof="sioc:Item foaf:Document">

      <span property="dc:title" content="Latin for Reading Knowledge" class="rdf-meta element-hidden"></span><span property="sioc:num_replies" content="0" datatype="xsd:integer" class="rdf-meta element-hidden"></span>
  
  <div class="content clearfix">
    <div class="field field-name-field-course-image field-type-image field-label-hidden"><div class="field-items"><div class="field-item even"><img typeof="foaf:Image" src="http://www.livesandletters.ac.uk/sites/default/files/styles/300px-square/public/latin_0.jpg?itok=Q4MacqFq" width="300" height="300" alt="" /></div></div></div><div class="field field-name-body field-type-text-with-summary field-label-hidden"><div class="field-items"><div class="field-item even" property="content:encoded"> <p>This module will provide an introduction to the grammar and vocabulary of Latin as it was used in the post-classical era, as a starting point for reading texts and documents from that&nbsp;period.</p>
<p>Students will come away from this module with a basic understanding of how the language works and some of the changes it underwent during the Middle Ages and the Renaissance. They will acquire a grasp of the fundamentals of Latin grammar, and a broad grounding in vocabulary, both general and the specialised language frequently encountered in historical source&nbsp;materials.</p>
<ul><li>Code: <span class="caps">EDITG003</span></li>
<li>Credit value:&nbsp;15 credits</li>
<li>Module tutor: <a href="mailto:jaap.geraerts.09@ucl.ac.uk">Dr Jaap&nbsp;Geraerts</a></li>
<li>Teaching: <span lang="EN-US" xml:lang="EN-US">Twenty 1-hour seminars </span><span lang="EN-US" xml:lang="EN-US">over two&nbsp;terms</span></li>
<li>Assessment: Two 1-hour written examinations (50%&nbsp;each)</li>
</ul></div></div></div>  </div>

  <div class="content clearfix">
    <div class="field field-name-field-course-image field-type-image field-label-hidden"><div class="field-items"><div class="field-item even"><img typeof="foaf:Image" src="http://www.livesandletters.ac.uk/sites/default/files/styles/300px-square/public/letter-447577_640.jpg?itok=AWL5V0bC" width="300" height="300" alt="letter" title="letter" /></div></div></div><div class="field field-name-body field-type-text-with-summary field-label-hidden"><div class="field-items"><div class="field-item even" property="content:encoded"> <p>This module teaches students how to analyse early modern epistolary conventions so as to be able to identify the extensive array of formal components required. This work is based on original archival materials and printed&nbsp;editions.</p>
<p>It instructs them in the close reading of early modern letters so as to extract elements relevant to narrative history and&nbsp;biography.</p>
<ul><li>Code: <span class="caps">EDITG004</span></li>
<li>Credit value:&nbsp;15 credits</li>
<li>Module tutor:&nbsp;t.b.c.</li>

<li>Assessment: One 3000 word essay&nbsp;(100%)</li>

  <div class="content clearfix">
    <div class="field field-name-field-course-image field-type-image field-label-hidden"><div class="field-items"><div class="field-item even"><img typeof="foaf:Image" src="http://www.livesandletters.ac.uk/sites/default/files/styles/300px-square/public/cell-students-prepare_0.jpg?itok=n56v90Pi" width="300" height="300" alt="" /></div></div></div><div class="field field-name-body field-type-text-with-summary field-label-hidden"><div class="field-items"><div class="field-item even" property="content:encoded"> <p>The Director’s Seminar provides a weekly opportunity for the <span class="caps">CELL</span> community to come together and share their work in a productive, supportive and convivial atmosphere. Our goal is to provide graduate students with an additional set of skills to enhance those provided in the graduate skills training sessions. All interested postgraduate students are welcome – whether you’re from <span class="caps"><span class="caps">CELL</span></span>, <span class="caps"><span class="caps">UCL</span></span>, or any other university.                     <h1 class="title" id="page-title">
          The Director&#039;s Seminar: First Term 2013/14        </h1>
                          <div class="tabs">
                  </div>
                          <div class="region region-content">
    <div id="block-system-main" class="block block-system">

    
  <div class="content">
    <div id="node-36" class="node node-course node-full clearfix" about="/courses/directors-seminar/2013-14-i" typeof="sioc:Item foaf:Document">

      <span property="dc:title" content="The Director&#039;s Seminar: First Term 2013/14" class="rdf-meta element-hidden"></span><span property="sioc:num_replies" content="0" datatype="xsd:integer" class="rdf-meta element-hidden"></span>
  
  <div class="content clearfix">
    <div class="field field-name-field-course-image field-type-image field-label-hidden"><div class="field-items"><div class="field-item even"><img typeof="foaf:Image" src="http://www.livesandletters.ac.uk/sites/default/files/styles/300px-square/public/cell-students-prepare.jpg?itok=PZB9Xgzn" width="300" height="300" alt="Cell Students" title="Cell Students" /></div></div></div><div class="field field-name-body field-type-text-with-summary field-label-hidden"><div class="field-items"><div class="field-item even" property="content:encoded"> <p>The <a href="/courses/directors-seminar">Director&#8217;s Seminar</a> (Thursdays, 10.30-12, Room 111 Foster Court) is a weekly gathering of graduate students and sta&#xfb00;, led by Professor Lisa Jardine. As well as welcoming a dynamic group of visiting speakers from pan-disciplinary &#xfb01;elds, we aim to discuss topics of all kinds related to the theory and practice of graduate life. Our goal is to provide graduate students with an additional set of skills to enhance those provided in the graduate skills training sessions. Please click ‘read more’ to see the sessions for autumn term 2013. All interested postgraduate students are welcome - whether you&#8217;re from <span class="caps">CELL</span>, <span class="caps">UCL</span>, or any other university. Please contact either <a href="robyn.adams@ucl.ac.uk">Robyn Adams</a> or <a href="m.symonds@ucl.ac.uk">Matthew Symonds</a> if you&#8217;d like to join&nbsp;in!</p>
<dl><dt><strong>26 September:</strong> Nydia Pineda (<span class="caps">QMUL</span>), <cite>Competing selenographies in Robert Hooke&#8217;s Micrographia </cite></dt>
<dd>A telescopic observation of the moon in a book about microscopy re&#xfb02;ects con&#xfb02;icting ideas about naming and representing the unknown in the Seventeenth-Century. My reading of this image allows a further understanding of lunar images as examples of visual technologies and as rhetorical tools for communicating expertise in shared or competing epistemological programs. These considerations shed further light on the agency of images in early modern scienti&#xfb01;c&nbsp;books.</dd>
</dl><dl><dt><strong>3 October:</strong> Will Tosh (<span class="caps">QMUL</span>), <cite>“Take all my loves, my love; yea, take them all”: the hidden romance of Elizabethan public life </cite></dt>
<dd>What stories do we miss when we &#xfb01;llet our public records for matters of state and leave aside the ‘personal stu&#xfb00;’? And should we be separating the two at&nbsp;all?.</dd>
</dl><dl><dt><strong>10 October:</strong> Sietske Fransen (Warburg Institute), <cite>The visual representation of knowledge in seventeenth-century medical practice </cite></dt>
<dd>This paper focuses on the dichotomous tables that comprise the manuscript <cite>Tabbulae morborum </cite>of a medical student from Trinity College, Cambridge, Daniel Foote (1629-1700). This manuscript, in combination with Foote’s personal note books and lists of herbals, gives us the rare opportunity to study the development of knowledge from the reception of a university education to medical&nbsp;practice.</dd>
</dl><dl><dt><strong>17 October:</strong> Katherine Hunt (<span class="caps">UEA</span>), <cite>Church bells and combinatory ideas of change in seventeenth-century England </cite></dt>
<dd>In the seventeenth-century English practice of change-ringing, church bells were rung in series of mathematical permutation. This paper analyses the relationship of this system to other combinatorial practices, particularly those to do with language, and examines how the contemporary use of the phrase ‘ringing the changes’ re&#xfb02;ected the concepts of variation and exhaustion on which such systems&nbsp;rely.</dd>
</dl><dl><dt><strong>24 October:</strong> Adam Parr (<span class="caps">UCL</span>), <cite>A soldier&#8217;s perspective on the Art of War: 1767 to 1775 </cite></dt>
<dd>In 1767 a British lieutenant of marines, John Clarke, published a translation of the &#xfb01;fth-century Latin text by Vegetius on the Art of War. Eight years later, Clarke himself wrote an eye-witness account of the battle of Bunker Hill. These two texts, and the di&#xfb00;erences between them, re&#xfb02;ect the revolutionary changes that took place in the world, Britain and warfare at this&nbsp;moment.</dd>
</dl><dl><dt><strong>31 October:</strong> Clare Whitehead (<span class="caps">QMUL</span>), ‘<cite>With a Kingdom&#8217;s happiness / Doth she privates Lares bless’: representing Anna of Denmark on her journey to London </cite></dt>
<dd>This paper examinea the rumours that surrounded Anna of Denmark&#8217;s illness in 1603 and the e&#xfb00;ect they had on performances given during her journey to London that&nbsp;year.</dd>
</dl><dl><dt><strong>14 November:</strong> Gillian Pink (Voltaire Foundation, University of Oxford), <cite>Spaces of annotation. Voltaire&#8217;s marginalia </cite></dt>
<dd>An examination of Voltaire’s practices as a writer of marginalia, with particular emphasis on the use of space and the annotated volume as a material&nbsp;object.</dd>
</dl><dl><dt><strong>21 November:</strong> To be&nbsp;con&#xfb01;rmed.</dt>
</dl><dl><dt><strong>28 November:</strong> James Everest (<span class="caps">UCL</span>), <cite>Thomas Hobbes&#8217;s Optical Instruments</cite></dt>
<dd>Famous as a political philosopher, Thomas Hobbes was also a natural philosopher, much of whose early work is devoted to the study of optics. A number of scholars have discussed various aspects of his theories about light and vision, but this paper foregrounds his practical experience with a range of optical instruments, hitherto almost completely&nbsp;ignored.</dd>
</dl><dl><dt><strong>5 December:</strong> Emma Pauncefort (<span class="caps">UCL</span>), <cite>Whetting or suppressing the appetite for English customs?: ‘Englishness’, food, drink and feasting in Misson&#8217;s 1698 Mémoires </cite></dt>
<dd>This paper examines how the French Huguenot refugee Henri Misson crafts the alphabetical entries in his 1698 <cite>Mémoires et Observations faites par un voyageur</cite>. As a case study, it considers how those entries concerning food, drink and feasting are manipulated to challenge long-entrenched stereotypes of the uncivilised and barbaric English whilst exhorting the readers – Englishmen and Frenchmen alike – to re-examine their approach to cultural&nbsp;di&#xfb00;erence.</dd>
</dl><dl><dt><strong>12 December:</strong> Helen Graham-Matheson (<span class="caps">UCL</span>), <cite>‘Land &#8230; is the only thing that lasts’: The three wives of William Parr and one small piece of land in Essex</cite></dt>
<dd>Through a vignette of the landholdings of Anne Bourchier, Elisabeth Cobham and Helena von Snakenbourg, the three wives of William Parr, marquess of Northampton, this paper explores women&#8217;s legal rights to property and landholdings across the mid-sixteenth century. I present the case of a small manor holding from the ancestral earldom of Essex as it passed through three women, across 45 years and appeared in legal transactions variously as a dowery, inheritance, forfeit, an enfeo&#xfb00;ment, and an&nbsp;exchange.</dd>
</dl></div></div></div>  </div>

                    <h1 class="title" id="page-title">
          The Director&#039;s Seminar: Second Term 2013/14        </h1>
                          <div class="tabs">
                  </div>
                          <div class="region region-content">
    <div id="block-system-main" class="block block-system">

    
  <div class="content">
    <div id="node-93" class="node node-page node-full clearfix" about="/courses/directors-seminar/2013-14-ii" typeof="foaf:Document">

      <span property="dc:title" content="The Director&#039;s Seminar: Second Term 2013/14" class="rdf-meta element-hidden"></span><span property="sioc:num_replies" content="0" datatype="xsd:integer" class="rdf-meta element-hidden"></span>
  
  <div class="content clearfix">
    <div class="field field-name-body field-type-text-with-summary field-label-hidden"><div class="field-items"><div class="field-item even" property="content:encoded"> <p>The <a href="/courses/directors-seminar">Director&#8217;s Seminar</a> (Thursdays, 10.30-12, Room 111 Foster Court) is a weekly gathering of graduate students and sta&#xfb00;, led by Professor Lisa Jardine. As well as welcoming a dynamic group of visiting speakers from pan-disciplinary &#xfb01;elds, we aim to discuss topics of all kinds related to the theory and practice of graduate life. Our goal is to provide graduate students with an additional set of skills to enhance those provided in the graduate skills training sessions. Please click ‘read more’ to see the sessions for autumn term 2013. All interested postgraduate students are welcome - whether you&#8217;re from <span class="caps">CELL</span>, <span class="caps">UCL</span>, or any other university. Please contact either <a href="mailto:robyn.adams@ucl.ac.uk">Robyn Adams</a> or <a href="mailto:m.symonds@ucl.ac.uk">Matthew Symonds</a> if you&#8217;d like to join&nbsp;in!</p>
<dl><dt><strong>16 January:</strong> <cite>Graphs, Maps, Trees and Other Ways of Understanding Archives and Books</cite></dt>
<dd>We will dedicate this seminar to discuss information management and di&#xfb00;erent ways of visualising and communicating our research. Anne Blair’s Too Much to Know and Franco Moretti’s Graphs, Maps, Trees will be our point of&nbsp;departure.</dd>
</dl><dl><dt><strong>23 January:</strong> <cite>Avoiding hairballs: a nuanced approach to mapping data in the humanities</cite></dt>
<dd>Building on current research work currently being conducted at <span class="caps">CELL</span>, this seminar will be a group discussion of best practice in applying data visualisation techniques to humanities&nbsp;research</dd>
</dl><dl><dt><strong>30 January:</strong> <cite>Tips for applying for funding</cite></dt>
<dd>A <span class="caps">CELL</span> workshop on the practical issues of academic research&nbsp;life</dd>
</dl><dl><dt><strong>6 February:</strong> <span class="caps">RSA</span> Panel Session 1, <cite>Rumour and reputation in early modern England</cite></dt>
<dd>Helen Graham-Matheson (<span class="caps">UCL</span>), Clare Whitehead (<span class="caps">QMUL</span>) and Nydia Pineda (<span class="caps">QMUL</span>) present papers examining the role of reputation and rumour in early modern politics and&nbsp;science</dd>
</dl><dl><dt><strong>13 February: </strong>Kate Maltby (<span class="caps">UCL</span>), <cite>Why Should We Study Lady Jane Lumley&#8217;s Greek?</cite></dt>
<dd>Jane Lumley&#8217;s translation of Iphigenia at Aulis is the &#xfb01;rst known translation of Euripides into English. Produced shortly after the execution of Lady Jane Grey, Lumley&#8217;s text drastically reshapes Euripides’ portrayal of a father&#8217;s responsibility for his daughter&#8217;s death. But before we can make historical claims about the reasons for Lumley&#8217;s adaptation of the text, we need to rule out technical error. This paper thus takes an introductory look at Lumley&#8217;s Greek, as a starting point for discussing the broader issues in scholarly analysis of Early Modern Greek and Latinity.<br /></dd>
</dl><dl><dt><strong><strong>27 February:</strong> </strong>Katie Bank (Royal Holloway), <cite>‘Musicke doth witnesse call’: representing truth and self in English vocal music</cite></dt>
<dd>This paper examines England’s di&#xfb03;cult reconciliation between old truths and new at a critical point surrounding 1600, through an interdisciplinary examination of William Byrd’s consort song setting of Philip Sidney’s <cite>O You that heare this voyce</cite>, placing music at the centre of the contrastive and morphing perceptions of truth in early seventeenth-century&nbsp;England.</dd>
</dl><dl><dt><strong>6 March: </strong>Heather Froehlich (University of Strathclyde), <cite>Though this be madness, yet there is a method in&#8217;t: &#xfb01;nding patterns of gender in Early Modern London plays</cite></dt>
<dd>This paper will discuss three binaries of gendered nouns in three increasingly larger corpora: a corpus of early modern drama, and all of <span class="caps">EEBO</span>. I address presence and absence of statistically-likely collocations from Shakespeare in the two larger corpora, suggesting ways digital approaches to text can help us identify these kinds of patterns (or lack&nbsp;thereof).</dd>
</dl><dl><dt><strong><strong>13 March:</strong></strong> <span class="caps">RSA</span> Panel Session 2, <cite>News, Margins, Libraries</cite></dt>
<dd>Kirsty Rolfe (<span class="caps">QMUL</span>), Matthew Symonds (<span class="caps">UCL</span>), Robyn Adams (<span class="caps">UCL</span>) and Brooke Palmieri (<span class="caps">UCL</span>) present papers focused on their current research into news networks, marginalia, and the Bodleian&nbsp;Library</dd>
</dl><dl><dt><strong><strong><strong><strong>20 March:</strong> </strong></strong></strong>Ruth Ahnert (<span class="caps">QMUL</span>), <cite>Tudor surveillance, meta-data, and the case of Edward Courtenay</cite></dt>
<dd>Reconstructing the networks of political power in Tudor England by mining the correspondence collected in the state papers archive (via State Papers Online), and analysing them using computational tools and algorithms developed within the &#xfb01;eld of complex networks, this paper demonstrates the highly connected network of Edward Courtenay in the reign of Mary I, and not only tells us some interesting things about the man, but also about the formation of the archive, and the uses of this methodology for the study of correspondence more&nbsp;generally.</dd>
</dl></div></div></div>  </div>

    <div class="field field-name-body field-type-text-with-summary field-label-hidden"><div class="field-items"><div class="field-item even" property="content:encoded"> <p>The <a href="/courses/directors-seminar">Director&#8217;s Seminar</a> (Thursdays, 10.30-12, Room 111 Foster Court) is a weekly gathering of graduate students and sta&#xfb00;, led by Professor Lisa Jardine. As well as welcoming a dynamic group of visiting speakers from pan-disciplinary &#xfb01;elds, we aim to discuss topics of all kinds related to the theory and practice of graduate life. Our goal is to provide graduate students with an additional set of skills to enhance those provided in the graduate skills training sessions. Please click ‘read more’ to see the sessions for autumn term 2014. All interested postgraduate students are welcome - whether you&#8217;re from <span class="caps">CELL</span>, <span class="caps">UCL</span>, or any other university. Please contact <a href="mailto:m.symonds@ucl.ac.uk">Matthew Symonds</a> if you&#8217;d like to join&nbsp;in!</p>
<dl><dt><strong>9 October</strong>: <cite>Public Speaking</cite></dt>
<dt>Professor Lisa Jardine (<span class="caps">UCL</span>) will discuss the art of public&nbsp;speaking</dt>
<dt> </dt>
<dt><strong>16 October</strong>:<cite>Practice run for upgrade</cite></dt>
<dt>Brooke Palmieri (<span class="caps">UCL</span>) will give a presentation in preparation for her PhD&nbsp;upgrade</dt>
<dt> </dt>
<dt><strong>23 October</strong>:<cite>Preparation for Failure in the Archives</cite></dt>
<dt>Delegating of duties and &#xfb01;nal preparations for the conference next&nbsp;week</dt>
<dt> </dt>
<dt><strong>30th October</strong>: <cite>Failure in the Archives conference</cite></dt>
<dt><a href="http://failureinthearchives.wordpress.com/">Read more</a> about the&nbsp;conference</dt>
<dt> </dt>
<dt><strong>6 November</strong>: <em>Reading&nbsp;week</em></dt>
<dt> </dt>
<dt><strong>13 November</strong>:<cite>‘Women&#8217;s academies and extracurricular education in Restoration London&#8217;</cite></dt>
<dt>A paper from John Gallagher (Gonville &amp; Caius,&nbsp;Cambridge)</dt>
<dt> </dt>
<dt><strong>20 November</strong>: <cite>No seminar </cite></dt>
<dt> </dt>
<dt><strong>27 November</strong>: <em>No&nbsp;seminar </em></dt>
<dt>‘<cite>Reading Between The Lines: John Dee’s Conversations With Angels’, a paper by Cassie Gorman, Cambridge</cite></dt>
<dt>This seminar has been <span class="caps">CANCELLED</span>. It will be rescheduled in the new&nbsp;year.</dt>
<dt> </dt>
<dt><strong>4 December</strong>: ‘<em>Orpheus the Ballad Seller? Changing Attitudes to Classical Mythology and the Powers of Music in Early Modern England’, a</em><em> paper by Katherine Butler,&nbsp;Oxford</em></dt>
<dt>Early modern apologists for music often drew extensively on classical mythology for exemplars of its distinguished history, powerful e&#xfb00;ects, and importance to society (particularly the tales of Orpheus, Amphion, and Arion). Interpreting the potential truths that were believed to lie behind the mythological &#xfb01;ctions was far from straightforward, however. Varying historical and allegorical methods might be applied, following traditions stretching back to classical antiquity. Allegorical explanations provided moral and philosophical justi&#xfb01;cations for music, while the historical tradition turned these stories into benchmarks against which the e&#xfb03;cacy of modern music was judged and found wanting. During the seventeenth century there were increasing attempts to provide rationalised interpretations for myths and the astonishing musical e&#xfb00;ects they described. Furthermore, the turn towards empiricism and experiment changed the status of myths as evidence of music’s e&#xfb00;ects. While the tradition of the powers of music was tenacious, some natural philosophers and music critics were beginning to question whether ancient music had achieved greater e&#xfb00;ects than contemporary music. Authors and playwrights became less reverent in their portrayals of mythical musical heroes. Moreover as their expectations of music’s powers declined some writers even began to question the importance of moving the passions, regarding music’s ability to give entertainment and pleasure as&nbsp;su&#xfb03;cient.</dt>
<dt> </dt>
<dt><strong>11 December</strong>: <em>John Wallis and readers of the Bodleian&nbsp;Library </em></dt>
<dt>Louisiane Ferlier (<span class="caps">UCL</span>) will give a paper on her current research into John&nbsp;Wallis</dt>

Please contact <a href="mailto:m.symonds@ucl.ac.uk">Matthew Symonds</a> if you&#8217;d like to join&nbsp;in!</p>
<p> <br /><strong>15 January</strong>: <em>Planning session</em><br /> <br /><strong>22 January</strong>: <em>To be con&#xfb01;rmed</em><br /> <br /><strong>29 January</strong>: <em>C</em><em><em>assie Gorman: ‘</em>Reading Between The Lines: John Dee’s Conversations With Angels&#8217;</em><br />This is a paper about a mysterious collection of lines on a sixteenth-century manuscript. Strange linear marks pervade the pages of John Dee’s Mysteriorum Libri, his account of the many alleged conversations with angels that took place between him, his ‘scryer’ Edward Kelley and a colourful cast of visiting spirits. The sensational anecdotes surrounding the text have tended to obscure its positive values: its &#xfb01;ne detail, an attempt to deliver a full report of the manner and location of the conversations, provides a rare (albeit one-sided) insight into the practical circumstances of an occult art. I suggest in this paper that Dee’s unusual linear marks are not merely punctuative, but serve to recreate the spatial and temporal conditions of the conversations they record. I also consider what these marks – and their imperfect reproduction in Méric Casaubon&#8217;s edition A True and Faithful Relation – reveal of the contribution of Euclidean mathematics to Dee’s occult&nbsp;practices.</p>
<p> <br /><strong>5th February</strong>: <em>Jennifer Bishop: “Livery company clerks in early modern&nbsp;London”</em></p>
<p> <br /><strong>12 February</strong>: <em>Erin Webster: ‘The “optics” of virtue in Aphra Behn’s </em>The Emperor of the Moon&#8217;<br />The subject of this talk is the “optics” of virtuous viewing in Aphra Behn’s understudied play, The Emperor of the Moon (1687), and their relation to the play&#8217;s satire of the experimental practices of the early Royal Society. In Behn’s play, the self-styled “virtuous” virtuoso Dr. Baliardo makes use of telescopes and microscopes to voyeuristically peep into private, domestic spaces, while simultaneously demanding that his female charges be “kept from the sight of man” so as to preserve their virtue. While the Royal Society has been identi&#xfb01;ed as a target of Behn’s satirical portrait of the doctor, the gender politics underlying this treatment have yet to be explored. Drawing on Robert Boyle&#8217;s Christian Virtuoso, Robert Hooke&#8217;s Micrographia and Thomas Sprat&#8217;s History of the Royal Society, I argue that Behn’s depiction of Dr. Baliardo is a pointed critique of the sexually-charged rhetoric of experimentalists like Boyle and Hooke, who posit their own virtuous, experimentalist eye as a means of containing and controlling a wanton, feminized, and sexualized&nbsp;“Nature.”</p>
<p><strong>19 February</strong>: <em>Reading week</em><br /> <br /><strong>26 February</strong>: <em>Lucy Judd, ‘Early Modern Receipt Books&#8217;</em><br />In recent years, scholars have begun to reassess the value of receipt book manuscripts. Previously, they had been largely dismissed by historians as a literary genre of women’s life-writing, or at best, viewed as a useful footnote to evidence common domestic practices within the early modern household. Sara Pennell, Michelle DiMeo, Elaine Leong, Linda Pollock and Jayne Archer have begun to highlight the more universal value of these manuscripts, and to alleviate some of the ‘academic scepticism’ associated with their study, particularly with reference to writing women’s history. Receipt book manuscripts have, under closer examination, been shown to provide a fascinating insight into both the cultural and intellectual pursuits of women in the early modern period as a whole, and as a result of these works, a signi&#xfb01;cant amount has begun to be understood in regards to the sphere of domestic knowledge at this&nbsp;time.</p>
<p>This paper will seek to explore recipe-writing and recipe-sharing networks through the lens of three local receipt book collections associated with the Nottinghamshire and Derbyshire regions.  Firstly, we will re&#xfb02;ect upon the varied styles and formats of receipt book compilations, as a way of understanding how and why these manuscripts were compiled and used. Secondly, we will begin to explore the knowledge-sharing networks of these local gentry-women, and to question the extent to which perceived contemporary gender boundaries remained prevalent in the sphere of early modern domestic knowledge-sharing. Finally, we will begin to highlight the broad spectrum of individuals involved in receipt donation, spanning social superiors to household&nbsp;servants.</p>
