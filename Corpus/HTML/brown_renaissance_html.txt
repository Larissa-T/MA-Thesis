<h4></h4>
<h4><strong>2017-2018 Courses in Renaissance and Early Modern Studies&nbsp;</strong></h4>
<p class="style4">&nbsp;</p>
<h3 class="style4 large"><strong><span class="style6">FALL 2017</span></strong></h3>
<p>The following courses may be of interest to Renaissance and Early Modern Studies concentrators. Please see the sponsoring department for the time and location of each course.</p>
<p><strong><span style="text-decoration:underline">Comparative Literature<br /></span></strong><span><strong>COLT 0610E </strong>- Crisis and Identity in Mexico, 1519-1968, S.Merrim<br /></span><span><strong>COLT 0810H</strong> - How Not to Be a Hero, K.Haynes</span></p>
<p><strong><span style="text-decoration:underline">English<br /></span></strong><span><strong>ENGL 1361G</strong> - Tolkien and the Renaissance, J.Kuzner<br /></span><span><strong>ENGL 1361H</strong> - Shakespearean Comedy, K.Newman</span></p>
<p><strong><span style="text-decoration:underline">French Studies<br /></span></strong><strong>FREN1410T</strong><span>&nbsp;- L'expérience des réfugiés/immigrés, V. Krause<br /></span><strong><span>F</span></strong><span><strong>REN 2130</strong> – Au-delà de </span>l’Europe<span>: La France et </span>le<span> </span>monde<span> </span>au<span> XVIIe siècle, L. <h3><strong>Graduate Student Activities</strong></h3>
<h3><strong><br /></strong></h3>
<ul>
<li>REMS and the Annmary Brown Memorial serve as the forum for a vibrant community of graduate students in affiliated departments. The REMS Graduate Colloquium meets several times a semester in the convivial setting of the Memorial, to hear and discuss papers by scholars or graduate students in an interdisciplinary context.</li>
</ul>
<h6 class="large no-bullet"></h6>
<h6 class="large no-bullet"></h6>
<h6 class="large no-bullet"></h6>
<h6 class="large no-bullet"></h6>

<h4>PaleoSlam with Leland Grigoli</h4>
<p>(a place to learn and/or practice our skill at reading premodern scripts)</p>
<p>Our default language will be Latin, but send along any vernacular examples you might have.&nbsp;<span>All levels of paleographic and linguistic proficiency are welcome; this is a place to learn from each other!</span></p>
<p><span>The meetings will (tentatively) be from&nbsp;</span><span class="aBn"><span class="aQJ">5:30</span></span><span>&nbsp;to 7 in Sharpe 107 on the following dates (all Wednesdays):</span></p>

<h3><strong>Graduate Student Activities</strong></h3>
<h3><strong><br /></strong></h3>
<ul>
<li>REMS and the Annmary Brown Memorial serve as the forum for a vibrant community of graduate students in affiliated departments. The REMS Graduate Colloquium meets several times a semester in the convivial setting of the Memorial, to hear and discuss papers by scholars or graduate students in an interdisciplinary context.</li>
</ul>
<h6 class="large no-bullet"></h6>
<h6 class="large no-bullet"></h6>
<h6 class="large no-bullet"></h6>
<h6 class="large no-bullet"></h6>

</ul>

<h4>PaleoSlam with Leland Grigoli</h4>
<p>(a place to learn and/or practice our skill at reading premodern scripts)</p>

        <h1 class="article-title  with-tabs">Renaissance Studies Graduate Students</h1>                        <div id="body-content" class="second-column column main-column">
                                                  <div class="clear-block"> 
<article id="node-246" class="main-article node">








  <div class="content">
        <p>The Program in Renaissance and Early Modern Studies does not have its own graduate program but it supports the graduate students from other departments working in the period. Below is a list of people who form a close-knit group of REMS grad students.</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p><strong><a href="mailto:Amiri_Ayanna@brown.edu" rel="nofollow">Amiri Ayanna</a></strong>'s&nbsp;<span style="color:#2f2a20">research focuses on women's agency and German vernacular&nbsp;authorship of the long fifteenth century. Her dissertation,&nbsp;“Reconsidering Gender and Genre: German Women Writers and their Readers, 1400-1550” &nbsp;examines laywomen and monastic authors' writing&nbsp;in the&nbsp;vernacular. Both groups of writers used "popular" and devotional genres to give voice to personal, familial, civic, and religious concerns within the narrative framework of&nbsp;Christian salvation. Her work has appeared in&nbsp;</span><em>Exemplaria</em><span style="color:#2f2a20">,&nbsp;</span><em>The Journal of Medieval Religious Cultures</em><span style="color:#2f2a20">,&nbsp;</span><em>Asymptote,&nbsp;</em><span style="color:#2f2a20">and DeGruyter's&nbsp;</span><em>Fundamentals of Medieval and Early Modern Cultures&nbsp;</em>book&nbsp;<span style="color:#2f2a20">series</span><em>.</em><span style="color:#2f2a20">&nbsp;She has received a PEN American Center Grant to support her translation of the&nbsp;</span><em>Saint Katharinental Sister Book of the Nuns at Diessenhofen.&nbsp;</em>Before coming to Brown, Amiri was a Fulbright Graduate Research Fellow at Ludwig-Maximilians-Universität in Munich. She also earned an M.T.S. degree from Harvard Divinity School, where her work focused on late medieval women's religious history in the upper Rhineland.</p>

She earned her Master’s degree in Art History from the University of Washington and her Bachelor of Arts in Art History from the University of Minnesota, Twin Cities. She takes as a starting point the premise that poetry was itself a project that involved figurative movement (pace, feet, the turn) and that the literature of the 1580s onward demonstrates that this project was concerned not only with the work of constructing metrical feet but also with conceiving of the ground on which these feet would make their mark.&nbsp; While she has a specific interest in travel narratives and ethnographies, the focus of her dissertation,&nbsp;</span><span>"Hybridity and the Mixed State: Avatars of Displacement in English Renaissance Literature,"</span><span>&nbsp;is on the ways in which various avatars of geographical displacement—knights errant, exiled figures, captives, and explorers—are deployed in literature that is not explicitly related to travel in order to trace the ways in which imagining England’s place in the world was an increasingly cosmopolitan project that recognized the limits of insularity and separation.</span><span>&nbsp;</span></li>

        <h1 class="article-title  with-tabs">Renaissance Studies Graduate Students</h1>                        <div id="body-content" class="second-column column main-column">
                                                  <div class="clear-block"> 
<article id="node-246" class="main-article node">








  <div class="content">
        <p>The Program in Renaissance and Early Modern Studies does not have its own graduate program but it supports the graduate students from other departments working in the period. Below is a list of people who form a close-knit group of REMS grad students.</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p><strong><a href="mailto:Amiri_Ayanna@brown.edu" rel="nofollow">Amiri Ayanna</a></strong>'s&nbsp;<span style="color:#2f2a20">research focuses on women's agency and German vernacular&nbsp;authorship of the long fifteenth century. Her dissertation,&nbsp;“Reconsidering Gender and Genre: German Women Writers and their Readers, 1400-1550” &nbsp;examines laywomen and monastic authors' writing&nbsp;in the&nbsp;vernacular. Both groups of writers used "popular" and devotional genres to give voice to personal, familial, civic, and religious concerns within the narrative framework of&nbsp;Christian salvation. Her work has appeared in&nbsp;</span><em>Exemplaria</em><span style="color:#2f2a20">,&nbsp;</span><em>The Journal of Medieval Religious Cultures</em><span style="color:#2f2a20">,&nbsp;</span><em>Asymptote,&nbsp;</em><span style="color:#2f2a20">and DeGruyter's&nbsp;</span><em>Fundamentals of Medieval and Early Modern Cultures&nbsp;</em>book&nbsp;<span style="color:#2f2a20">series</span><em>.</em><span style="color:#2f2a20">&nbsp;She has received a PEN American Center Grant to support her translation of the&nbsp;</span><em>Saint Katharinental Sister Book of the Nuns at Diessenhofen.&nbsp;</em>Before coming to Brown, Amiri was a Fulbright Graduate Research Fellow at Ludwig-Maximilians-Universität in Munich. She also earned an M.T.S. degree from Harvard Divinity School, where her work focused on late medieval women's religious history in the upper Rhineland.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong><a href="mailto:Charlotte_Buecheler@brown.edu" rel="nofollow">Charlotte Buecheler</a></strong><span> received her Licence de Lettres Modernes from Université Paris-Sorbonne Nouvelle in 2009 and </span><span>M.A. in Comparative Literature from Brown University in 2015. Her r</span><span>esearch Interests include Early Modern French and English Literatures; Narrative and Reader-Response Theories; Fictionality; Disability Studies. She speaks&nbsp;</span><span>French, English, German, Latin and Italian.</span></p>
</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>
<p><span>&nbsp;</span><strong><a href="mailto:Suzanne_Duff@brown.edu" rel="nofollow">Suzanne Duff</a></strong><span><a href="mailto:Suzanne_Duff@brown.edu" rel="nofollow"> </a>is currently a Ph.D. student at Brown University in the History of Art and Architecture. Her dissertation topic concerns the role of the St. Luke’s guild in early modern Antwerp in shaping the artistic identity of artists through the guild’s material culture and social networks. She earned her Master’s degree in Art History from the University of Washington and her Bachelor of Arts in Art History from the University of Minnesota, Twin Cities. Suzanne’s research interests include the social history of the artist in early modern Netherlands, print culture, craft guilds, markets for art, and the circulation of knowledge.</span></p>
</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>
<p><span>&nbsp;</span><strong><a href="mailto:Justin_Gibson@brown.edu" rel="nofollow">Justin Gibson</a></strong><span> is a PhD student in the Department of French Studies. He&nbsp; holds&nbsp;a BA in French from Eastern Kentucky University and an MA in French from Middlebury College. H is research interests include the&nbsp;guest/host relationship in Renaissance literature, &nbsp;the early modern reception of classical epic hospitality, phenomenology, and ecocriticism. </span></p>
</li>
<li>
<p><span><strong><span><a href="mailto:Christopher_Gillett@brown.edu" rel="nofollow">Christopher Gillett</a></span></strong><span>&nbsp;is a Ph.D. Candidate in the History Department at Brown University and a former NEH Research Fellow at the Center for Medieval and Renaissance Studies at St. Louis University. He has held a number of competitively awarded research grants and fellowships, including most recently a Fletcher Jones Foundation fellowship at the Huntington Library and a Short-Term Fellowship at the New York Public Library. His dissertation project seeks to reintegrate British and Irish Catholics into examinations of the development of revolutionary ideologies in the British Atlantic between 1630 and 1673, both by exploring the direct effects of Catholics attempts to renegotiate the Oath of Allegiance and by examining how Protestant groups hostile to such developments discovered and utilized information about these negotiations to formulate anti-popish, revolutionary rhetoric.</span><br /></span></p>
</li>
</ul>
<ul>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><strong><a href="mailto:Wanda_Henry@brown.edu" rel="nofollow">Wanda S. Henry</a></strong> held the Helen Childs Boyden Chair for Distinguished Teaching at Deerfield Academy where she taught mathematics and served as department chair and assistant dean of faculty.&nbsp; She also taught mathematics at Mt Holyoke College before turning to the study of history at Brown University.&nbsp; While at Brown, she has held the Peter Green Fellowship for Graduate Study and served as a Brown-Wheaton Faculty Fellow.&nbsp; She will continue to teach at Wheaton College while finishing her dissertation on the bills of mortality and the women who searched and buried the dead in early modern England. &nbsp; &nbsp;<span>&nbsp;</span></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><strong><a href="mailto:Talya_Housman@brown.edu" rel="nofollow">Talya Housman</a></strong>&nbsp;<span>Talya Housman is a PhD candidate in the History Department at Brown University. She holds a masters degree from Brown in History and a first class honors bachelor's degree from Trinity College Dublin in History and Political Science. Talya was the co-editor in chief of an edition of Henry Oxinden's miscellany published by the Folger Shakespeare Library in 2014. Her dissertation looks at the intersection between rape and ideas of legitimate power, ownership and force in the English Civil Wars and the Interregnum. The project exploring the diversion between different fear narratives peddled by various groups in public print and actual the experiences of women in this period.</span></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><span><strong><a href="mailto:Liise_Lehtsalu@brown.edu" rel="nofollow">Liise Lehtsalu</a></strong> is a PhD Candidate in the History Department. Her research focuses on the place of women's monastic institutions in&nbsp;early modern&nbsp;Italian society. Her PhD dissertation "Negotiated Lives: Third Order Women Religious and Their Communities in Seventeenth- and Eighteenth-Century Italy" focuses on the little-studied early modern history of women's third orders to&nbsp;expand significantly&nbsp;our understanding of&nbsp;early modern female monasticism and early modern women's lived experience. Her most recent work examines women religious in early modern Italian economy.&nbsp;<br /></span></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><strong><a href="mailto:Ali_Madani@brown.edu" rel="nofollow">Ali John Madani</a></strong><span>is a PhD student in the English department at Brown. His work centers on the literature and culture of the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries in England with special attention paid to the writing and cultural status of Shakespeare, Donne, and Milton. Questions concerning early modern conceptions of history and the ways in which contemporary criticism engages historical objects frame his research. Before arriving at Brown, Ali held a Benjamin Franklin fellowship for graduate study at the University of Pennsylvania.&nbsp;</span></li>
</ul>
<ul>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><span style="color:#2f2a20"><a href="mailto:erika_valdivieso@brown.edu" rel="nofollow">Erika Valdivieso&nbsp;</a></span><span>received her B.A. in Classics from the University of Michigan in 2011, graduating summa cum laude and with highest distinction. She received her Master's in Latin with Secondary Teaching Certification in 2014 from Michigan. At Brown, Erika is a Presidential Fellow. &nbsp;She is interested in imperial Latin literature and the reception of imperial ideology in the New World. &nbsp;Erika completed a Latin special author on Virgil and the Virgilian tradition with Andrew Laird. &nbsp;Her Greek special topic will be on ancient literary criticism with Johanna Hanink. &nbsp;Recent work has taken her into the reception of Plato's Symposium in the Renaissance, with a forthcoming publication in The Classical Tradition in Latin America. &nbsp;</span></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><strong><a href="mailto:Marley-Vincent_Lindsey@brown.edu" rel="nofollow">Marley-Vincent Lindsey</a></strong><span> is a History PhD student working in the Hispanic Atlantic World. He is currently interested in tracing the transmission of labor institutions like the&nbsp;</span><em>repartimiento</em><span>&nbsp;system and practices like the&nbsp;</span><em>limpieza de sangre</em><span>&nbsp;from late Medieval Castile into sixteenth century New Spain and the Caribbean. He's also working with digital methods in his research and writing about digital culture in the twenty first century. Prior to Brown, Marley was an undergraduate at the University of Chicago and spent time at the Newberry Library in Chicago and the General Archive of the Indies in Seville.&nbsp;</span><span>&nbsp;</span><span>&nbsp;</span></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><span><strong><a href="https://mail.google.com/mail/?view=cm&amp;fs=1&amp;tf=1&amp;to=diego_luis@brown.edu" target="_blank" title="Diego Luis">Diego Luis</a>&nbsp;</strong><span>s a first-year doctoral student who came to Brown to study 16th Century Spanish colonial exploration and popular knowledge of it.&nbsp;</span><span>Research Interests: Early Spanish conquests in the Americas.</span></span></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><strong><a href="https://www.brown.edu/academics/english/ali-john-madani">Ali John Madani</a></strong><span>'s r</span><span>esearch interests:&nbsp;Literary and Cultural Theory, Poetry and Poetics, Renaissance, Medieval, and Early Modern Literatures.</span></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><strong><a href="mailto:Emily_Monty@brown.edu" rel="nofollow">Emily Monty</a></strong><span> joined the graduate program in the History of Art and Architecture at Brown in 2013 with a focus in Cinquecento Italy. Her research investigates cultural interchange and mobility in the Renaissance, focusing in particular on paintings, engravings, and treatises as sites of knowledge exchange among Italian and Spanish artists at the end of the sixteenth-century. She completed her Master’s in the History of Art at Tufts University in 2012, where she wrote a thesis entitled “Re-forming Translation: Illustrated Scripture in Post-Tridentine Europe.” Her honors thesis (Bates College, 2010), “Lorenzo Ghiberti, Tradition, and Identity: the use of Byzantine prototypes in the north doors for the Florentine Baptistery,” looked at the survival of medieval iconography and style in the early Renaissance.&nbsp;</span><span>&nbsp;</span></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><strong><a href="https://www.brown.edu/academics/english/hilary-rasch">Hilary Rasch</a></strong><span>'s r</span><span>esearch interests include Gender and Sexuality Studies; Poetry and Poetics; Queer Theory/Gay and Lesbian Studies; Renaissance, Medieval, and Early Modern Literatures; Affect Theory; Body Studies</span></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><strong><a href="mailto:carmen_urbita_ibarreta@brown.edu" rel="nofollow">Carmen Urbita&nbsp;Ibarreta</a></strong><strong>&nbsp;&nbsp;</strong><span>is a Ph.D. student from Spain. She holds a BA in Comparative Literature from King's College London and completed her MSt in Modern Languages (Spanish &amp; French) at Oxford University. Among her research interests are early modern peninsular and colonial culture, early modern women's writing, reading and writing practices and the body.</span></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><strong><a href="mailto:Kelly_Whitford@brown.edu" rel="nofollow">Kelly Whitford</a></strong><span><a href="mailto:Kelly_Whitford@brown.edu" rel="nofollow">&nbsp;</a><span>received her B.A in Art History from Carleton College (2006) and her M.A. in Art History from the University of Oregon (2011). Her work focuses on Rome during the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries. Specifically, Kelly’s research examines Church rituals and ceremonies (especially pilgrimage), and the ways in which these performances incorporated statues, architecture, and public space. Her dissertation, "Embodying Belief: Crossing the Ponte Sant'Angelo with Bernini's Angels" uses Bernini’s marble sculptures as a fulcrum for thinking about the relationship of art and audience in early modern Rome and the embodied nature of beholding.</span><a href="mailto:Kelly_Whitford@brown.edu" rel="nofollow"></a></span></li>
</ul>
<ul>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><strong><a href="mailto:Christopher_Yates@brown.edu" rel="nofollow">Christopher Yates</a></strong><span>&nbsp;graduated from Cornell University in 2014 and is currently enrolled in his third year of the English PhD program at Brown. His research and writing center on questions of generation - economic, sexual, and linguistic - and the circulation of these resources in the English body politic. He is particularly interested in the themes of lineage and potency in Shakespeare's history plays, as well as the urban and social dimensions of Jacobean city comedy.&nbsp;</span></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><span><strong><a href="mailto:Minta_Zlomke@brown.edu" rel="nofollow">Minta Zlomke</a></strong> is a PhD candidate in the English Department at Brown University studying sixteenth and seventeenth century English literature.&nbsp; Her current work examines the role of travel and the fruits of exploration, colonization, and trade in the development of English literature. She takes as a starting point the premise that poetry was itself a project that involved figurative movement (pace, feet, the turn) and that the literature of the 1580s onward demonstrates that this project was concerned not only with the work of constructing metrical feet but also with conceiving of the ground on which these feet would make their mark.&nbsp; While she has a specific interest in travel narratives and ethnographies, the focus of her dissertation,&nbsp;</span><span>"Hybridity and the Mixed State: Avatars of Displacement in English Renaissance Literature,"</span><span>&nbsp;is on the ways in which various avatars of geographical displacement—knights errant, exiled figures, captives, and explorers—are deployed in literature that is not explicitly related to travel in order to trace the ways in which imagining England’s place in the world was an increasingly cosmopolitan project that recognized the limits of insularity and separation.</span><span>&nbsp;</span></li>
</ul>
